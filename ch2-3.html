Chapter 2: Briefly define:
  1. Natural language: The language we speak and write in our everyday lives.
  2. Pseudocode: This is a set of English-language constructs designed to more or less resemble statements in a programming language but that do not actually run on a computer.
  3. Sequential Operation (straight-line algorithm): Our pseudocode must include instructions to carry out the three basic sequential operations called computation, input, and output.
  4. Input: operations provide the computing agent with data values from the outside world that it may then use in later instructions.
  5. Output: operations send results from the computing agent to the outside world.
  6. Conditional Statements: are the “question-asking” operations of an algorithm.
  7. Boolean Condition: Basically, the if/then/else statement allows you to select exactly one of two alternatives—either/or, this or that.
  8. Algorithmic Discovery Process: Finding a solution to a given problem.

Chapter 3: Briefly describe or define:
  1. Why algorithms should be correct?
    An elegant and efficient algorithm that gives wrong results for the problem at hand is worse than useless. It can lead to mistakes that are enormously expensive or even fatal.
  2. Why algorithms should be efficient?
    Algorithms should be efficient due to time and space not being unlimited resources.
  3. Program maintenance:
    After a program is written, it needs to be maintained, both to fix any errors that are uncovered through repeated usage with different input values and to extend the program to meet new requirements.
  4. Importance of algorithm: ease of understanding
    Sometimes elegance and ease of understanding work at cross-purposes; the more elegant the solution, the more difficult it may be to understand
  5. algorithmic elegance:
    is a highly desirable characteristic of an algorithm.

Karl Friedrich Gauss

  1. Give a brief overview of Carl Friedrich Gauss' mathematical abilities and scientific achievements.
    Gauss is generally regarded as one of the greatest mathematicians of all time for his contributions to number theory, geometry, probability theory, geodesy, planetary astronomy, the theory of functions, and potential theory (including electromagnetism).
  2. Describe Karl Frederich Gauss" algorithm for adding up the number from 1 to 100.
    Gauss recognized he had fifty pairs of numbers when he added the first and last number in the series, the second and second-last number in the series, and so on.
  3. What are gaussian optics?
    Gaussian optics is a framework for describing optical phenomena, which is based on geometrical optics (ray optics) and makes extensive use of the paraxial approximation.
  4. What is a "guassian blur" and how is it used in digital image editing?
    The Gaussian blur feature is obtained by blurring (smoothing) an image using a Gaussian function to reduce the noise level.
    Blurring is a commonly used visual effect when digitally editing photos and videos.
